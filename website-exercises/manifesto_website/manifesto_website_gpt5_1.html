Incantations (10)

Short verbal formulas and charms attested across cultures. Sources noted where available.

  1. Vade retro Satana — Greater Key of Solomon (medieval grimoire); used as an exorcism/repelling formula.
  2. Abracadabra (diminishing charm) — Recorded by Serenus Sammonicus (3rd c.) and later adopted in medieval medicine to treat fevers.
  3. Hagios, Hagios, Hagios — Byzantine liturgical invocation (thrice-holy), adapted in Christian exorcisms and charms.
  4. Names of God sequence (e.g., Tetagrammaton) — Used in Kabbalistic and Solomonic texts to bind or command spirits.
  5. Egyptian heka recitations — Spells from the Egyptian Book of the Dead invoking gods such as Osiris and Sekhmet for protection.
  6. Arabic tutelary phrases (invoking divine names) — Found in medieval Islamic healing and protective talismans (e.g., names like Al‑Qadir).
  7. Norse oath-formulae — Runic and spoken bindings invoked by skalds and seers to seal vows (sagas and eddas).
  8. Carmina magica — Short Latin incantations in medieval grimoires for controlling minor spirits or gaining favor.
  9. Charm against the Evil Eye — Mediterranean and Near Eastern traditions using specific words plus symbolic gestures.
  10. Litany of Saints (protective variant) — Christian protective litany adapted into charms in medieval Europe.

Potions & Recipes (10)

Practical mixtures recorded in herbals, medical compendia and magical manuals; many are medicinal as well as ritual.

  1. Love Philtre — Rose petals, honey, wine, and a personal token; recipes appear in late medieval English manuscripts.
  2. Protection Draught — Mandrake and aromatic herbs in beer; attested in Egyptian and later medieval sources for warding.
  3. Sleep Elixir — Poppy (opium), chamomile and wine; remedies for insomnia in Dioscorides and medieval herbals.
  4. Distilled Waters (e.g., Aqua Vitae) — Early distillates used both medicinally and ritually in medieval/alchemical texts.
  5. Panacea-style compound — Galenic compound recipes mixing multiple spices and substances for broad cures.
  6. Invisibility / Stealth Anointment — Picatrix and other Arabic occult works list oils and applications tied to planetary hours.
  7. Embalming Unguent — Natron, myrrh and resin blends from Egyptian mortuary practices to preserve bodies.
  8. Venefic Charm-Drink — Herbal poisons and contrived draughts discussed in medieval toxicology and manuals.
  9. Vitriol / Alchemical Tinctures — Paracelsian and medieval alchemical recipes aiming at transformation or subtle effects.
  10. Draught for Bold Speech — Mead or wine infused with rowan, juniper, or runic-charged herbs used in Germanic traditions.

Divinations & Prophecies (10)

Historically attested techniques for discerning hidden information or future outcomes.

  1. Auspicy (avian signs) — Roman augury reading birds' flight; detailed in classical authors like Pliny.
  2. Haruspicy (entrail reading) — Etruscan and Roman practice of reading animal organs; recorded in Roman religious law and augural texts.
  3. Oracle consultation (e.g., Delphi) — Pythia and priestly interpretation of cryptic utterances in ancient Greece (Herodotus, Pausanias).
  4. Sortes (e.g., Virgilian sortes) — Randomly chosen lines from revered texts used in late antiquity and the medieval period.
  5. Geomancy — Arabic-derived method (Al-Kindi, 9th c.) using dot patterns to form interpretive figures.
  6. Astrology / Natal charts — Ptolemaic tradition and medieval astrologers used planetary positions to infer fate (Tetrabiblos).
  7. Cleromancy / Casting lots — Casting marked stones or lots; attested across the ancient Near East and biblical literature.
  8. Scrying (mirrors, bowls) — Gazing techniques in medieval grimoires and ancient folk practices using reflective surfaces.
  9. Onomancy / Gematria — Kabbalistic and numerological name-analysis used to reveal attributes or fates.
  10. Tarot/Cartomancy (early forms) — Card-based omens emerging in Renaissance Italy and later developed into modern tarot practices.

Historically informed summaries drawn from primary and secondary sources (Classical authors, medieval grimoires, herbals, and early modern alchemical works).
