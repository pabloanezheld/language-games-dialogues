Magic Incantations: A Brief Historical Guide

Incantations Potions Divinations & Prophecies

This page offers a simple overview of traditional spells, recipes and prophetic practices drawn from historical sources such as medieval grimoires, ancient Egyptian texts and Renaissance manuals.

Incantations

Historically, incantations were verbal formulas spoken aloud, often in Latin or a localized dialect, believed to carry power when pronounced correctly. Examples include:

  • Exorcism Formula – From the Greater Key of Solomon, a 14th‑century grimoire: Vade retro Satana; nunquam suade mihi vana... used to repel demons.
  • Arabic Charm – Inscribed in the 11th-century Book of Healing by Avicenna: Ya qahhar, ya qadir, invoking divine names to ward off illness.
  • Etruscan Blessing – Found in tomb inscriptions: alaleu, alaleu, a repeated cry meant to protect the dead on their journey.

Potions

Recipes for potions appear in herbals and magical texts, often combining plants, animal parts and astrological timing.

  • Love Philtre – A recipe from a 15th‑century English manuscript: rose petals, honey, and a hair of the beloved steeped in wine under the full moon.
  • Protection Draught – In the Papyrus Harris of ancient Egypt, a mixture of mandrake, snake marrow and beer was drunk to guard against evil spirits.
  • Sleep Elixir – Listed in Dioscorides' De materia medica: poppy juice, chamomile and wine, used to induce rest.

Divinations & Prophecies

Methods of foretelling the future varied widely; here are a few historically documented practices:

  • Auspicy – Roman priests (auspices) read the flight of birds; Pliny the Elder describes auspices as signs from Jupiter.
  • Lot Casting – The Sortes Virgilianae (choosing lines from Vergil at random) was used in the Middle Ages to gain prophetic insight.
  • Oracle of Delphi – In ancient Greece, the Pythia inhaled vapors and uttered cryptic pronouncements interpreted by priests, as described by Herodotus.
